Our History

When Did Our Story Begin?

The Vincent Atelier traces its origins to 1887, founded in the heart of old Manila by Don Alejandro Vincent as a sanctuary for art, scholarship, and cultural dialogue. What began as a private studio has evolved into one of the country’s most enduring institutions—surviving wars, relocations, and generations of change. Today, The Vincent Atelier continues its mission to preserve, present, and promote the artistic heritage of the Philippines and beyond.

Through colonial rule, modernization, and even the ravages of war, The Vincent Atelier endured—preserving fragments of beauty and resilience along the way. Relocated to San Lorenzo Village in Makati City after World War II, the institution continues to honor its founding vision by championing heritage, fostering dialogue, and inspiring future generations through the transformative power of art.

How Did The Collection Grow?

The Vincent Atelier began in a single room with fewer than 50 items — including 12 European sketches, 8 rare prints, and over 25 handwritten letters collected by Don Alejandro Vincent. As artists and intellectuals gathered at the Atelier, the collection expanded quickly, reflecting both local traditions and global influences.

By the 1930s, it housed over 600 artworks, ranging from classical oil paintings to indigenous textiles and modernist sculptures. Though many were lost during World War II, around 200 pieces were recovered and preserved. Today, The Vincent Atelier preserves and showcases more than 1,500 pieces, each representing a unique story, tradition, or innovation. The collection continues to grow through donations, acquisitions, and community engagement, ensuring that it remains both a guardian of history and a catalyst for future creativity.

Where Was The Collection Housed?

In its earliest days, the collection was housed in Don Alejandro Vincent's private studio near Plaza Moriones in Tondo, Manila. As public interest grew, the Atelier opened its first formal gallery in 1906 along Escolta Street, followed by an annex in Ermita dedicated to indigenous and contemporary works. By the 1930s, additional spaces in Santa Ana and near the University Belt were established, offering expanded access and educational programs. However, much of this infrastructure was destroyed during World War II. In 1948, the collection found a new and permanent home in San Lorenzo Village, Makati City, where it was rebuilt with care, blending traditional Filipino architecture with a modern vision for preservation and public engagement.
